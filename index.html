Decline of Black Farm Ownership

Black farm ownership throughout southern states has drastically declined since the late 1800s. This fact is highlighted in the 1901 Du Bois census bureau data, which reported that there were approximately 187,799 farms in the United States fully owned and operated by black farmers in 1900[1]. By 1910, 45 years after Emancipation, black farmers had acquired over 15 million acres of farmland and controlled approximately 218,000 Black farms [3]. However, due to severe systemic discrimination, heir's property, and discriminatory lending policies [2], there has been almost an 85% decrease in black farm ownership. Today, out of the 1, 900,487 farms in the U.S., only 1.71% or 32,653, are black-owned, accounting for only 0.60% of farmland in the country [3].

Importance of Black Centennial Farm Award Winners

Despite these challenges, Black farmers have historically been some of the most economically independent leaders in their communities. The Georgia Centennial Farm Program was developed in 1993 to distinguish family farms that have contributed to preserving Georgia's agricultural history. To apply for the award, farms must be at least 100 years old, at least 10 acres, and earn an annual income of at least $1,000 [4,5]. Out of over 600 winners in the program since its inception, only 15 have been black.
